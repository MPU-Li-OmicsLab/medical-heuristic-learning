| Feature | Univariate signal (summary) | Clinical rationale | Suggested threshold | Evidence confidence (high/medium/low) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| gender | Male sex is often associated with slightly higher in-hospital mortality in critical illness. | Males have a higher baseline risk for many life-threatening conditions (e.g., cardiovascular disease, sepsis) and may present with more severe disease. | Male (binary) | medium |
| age | Increasing age is one of the strongest predictors of hospital mortality. | Age-related decline in organ reserve, immune function (immunosenescence), and higher comorbidity burden directly increase vulnerability to adverse outcomes. | age > 65 years | high |
| Respiratory_failure | Presence of respiratory failure strongly increases mortality risk. | Respiratory failure indicates severe impairment of gas exchange, often requiring invasive mechanical ventilation, and reflects multi-organ vulnerability. | 1 (present) | high |
| ARDS | ARDS is a severe condition with high attributable mortality. | Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome involves diffuse alveolar damage, refractory hypoxemia, and a systemic inflammatory cascade, markedly raising the risk of death. | 1 (present) | high |
| sepsis | Sepsis is a major driver of in-hospital mortality. | Sepsis is life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection; it frequently leads to shock, multi-organ failure, and death. | 1 (present) | high |
| Ccoronaryheart_disease | History of coronary heart disease moderately increases mortality. | Reduced cardiac reserve and increased susceptibility to ischemia during systemic stress (e.g., sepsis, hypotension) elevate the risk of cardiac decompensation and death. | 1 (present) | medium |
| Hypertension | The association between hypertension and in-hospital mortality is inconsistent and often confounded. | Chronic hypertension causes end-organ damage, but in acute illness blood pressure may be artificially high or low; hypertension history alone is a weak independent predictor. | no clear threshold (binary “present” not reliably discriminative) | low |
| Diabetes | Diabetes mellitus is an established risk factor for higher hospital mortality. | Diabetes impairs immune function, delays wound healing, predisposes to infections and cardiovascular events, and is linked to worse outcomes in sepsis and acute respiratory failure. | 1 (present) | high |
| Kidney_failure | Chronic kidney disease/failure is a strong predictor of mortality. | Reduced renal clearance, electrolyte disturbances, fluid overload, uremic immunosuppression, and higher cardiovascular comorbidity substantially increase death risk in the hospital. | 1 (present) | high |
| Hyperlipidaemia | Hyperlipidaemia is not a consistent independent predictor; may show a paradoxical “protective” signal. | The association is heavily confounded by statin use, nutritional status, and reverse causality; it cannot be reliably used as a mortality predictor. | no clear threshold (binary “present” not discriminative) | low |
| COPD | COPD is a well-known risk factor for higher mortality. | Limited pulmonary reserve, chronic inflammation, higher risk of respiratory failure and pneumonia, and frequent exacerbations increase the likelihood of death during acute illness. | 1 (present) | high |
| Cirrhosis | Liver cirrhosis dramatically increases in-hospital mortality. | Cirrhosis impairs synthesis of coagulation factors, increases infection risk (spontaneous bacterial peritonitis), and predisposes to variceal bleeding, hepatic encephalopathy, and multi-organ failure. | 1 (present) | high |
| Hepatic_failure | Acute or chronic hepatic failure carries an extremely high mortality. | Loss of synthetic and detoxifying liver function leads to coagulopathy, encephalopathy, hypoglycemia, and severe metabolic derangements, often culminating in multi-organ failure. | 1 (present) | high |
| Fatty_liver | Fatty liver disease is not a robust independent predictor of acute hospital mortality. | While associated with metabolic syndrome, its direct short-term impact on in-hospital survival is weak and often masked by confounders like obesity and diabetes. | no clear threshold (binary “present” not reliably discriminative) | low |
| Acute_pancreatitis | Acute pancreatitis is associated with significantly increased mortality when severe. | Pancreatic inflammation can trigger systemic inflammatory response syndrome, third-spacing of fluids, acute kidney injury, and respiratory failure; severe cases have high mortality. | 1 (present) | medium |
| Cerebral_infarction | History of ischemic stroke is linked to higher mortality during acute illness. | Neurological deficits increase the risk of aspiration, immobility, and complications; stroke reflects advanced vascular disease, which worsens resilience to acute stress. | 1 (present) | medium |
| ICH | Intracranial hemorrhage is a catastrophic condition with very high early mortality. | ICH causes direct brain injury, elevated intracranial pressure, and herniation; it frequently leads to death or devastating neurological outcomes. | 1 (present) | high |
| Malignancy | Active malignancy is a powerful negative prognostic factor. | Cancer confers immunosuppression, hypercoagulability, poor nutritional status, and limited physiological reserve, all of which markedly increase hospital mortality. | 1 (present) | high |
| Solidtumors | Presence of solid tumors increases mortality. | Solid tumors cause direct organ dysfunction, metastatic complications, and paraneoplastic syndromes; they are a hallmark of advanced disease with limited reserve. | 1 (present) | high |
| heart_rate | Tachycardia is a sensitive but non-specific marker of physiological stress and worse outcome. | Elevated heart rate reflects compensatory mechanisms for hypovolemia, fever, pain, or early shock; sustained tachycardia (>100 bpm) indicates increased risk of decompensation. | heart_rate > 100 bpm | high |
| dbp | Low diastolic blood pressure points to shock or severe vasodilation. | DBP < 60 mmHg often signals inadequate coronary perfusion and systemic hypoperfusion, a common pre-terminal finding. | dbp < 60 mmHg | high |
| mbp | Low mean blood pressure defines hypotension and predicts mortality. | MAP < 65 mmHg is the widely accepted threshold for impaired organ perfusion; sustained values below this level cause ischemic injury and organ failure. | mbp < 65 mmHg | high |
| resp_rate | Tachypnea is an early and strong warning sign of critical illness. | Respiratory rate > 22 breaths/min is a component of early warning scores (e.g., qSOFA, NEWS); it indicates respiratory distress, metabolic acidosis, or sepsis. | resp_rate > 22 breaths/min | high |
| temperature | Both hypothermia and high fever signal severe underlying disease; hypothermia is especially ominous. | Temperature < 36 °C often reflects advanced sepsis, shock, or thermoregulatory failure, and is associated with higher mortality than fever alone. | temperature < 36.0 °C or > 38.5 °C | high |
| SOFA | Sequential Organ Failure Assessment score is the reference for organ dysfunction quantification. | SOFA ≥ 2 defines organ dysfunction in sepsis and is linearly associated with mortality; higher scores directly measure multi-organ failure severity. | SOFA ≥ 2 | high |
| caclium | Hypocalcemia is common in critical illness and linked to adverse outcomes. | Low ionized calcium impairs myocardial contractility and coagulation; total calcium < 2.1 mmol/L (≈8.4 mg/dL) indicates poorer prognosis. | calcium < 2.1 mmol/L (8.4 mg/dL) | medium |
| bicarbonate | Low bicarbonate concentration is a marker of metabolic acidosis and high mortality. | Bicarbonate < 22 mmol/L reflects lactic acidosis or renal failure; acidosis causes myocardial depression and catecholamine resistance. | bicarbonate < 22 mmol/L | high |
| Chloride | Extreme chloride levels, especially hyperchloremia, may be associated with worse outcomes. | Hyperchloremia (>110 mmol/L) is linked to renal vasoconstriction, acidosis, and increased mortality in ICU settings, though evidence is observational. | Chloride > 110 mmol/L | medium |
| Sodium | Dysnatremia (both hypo- and hypernatremia) is a consistent predictor of mortality. | Sodium < 135 or > 145 mmol/L reflects underlying osmotic disturbances (SIADH, dehydration, renal loss) and is independently associated with higher death rates. | Sodium < 135 mmol/L or > 145 mmol/L | high |
| Potassium | Deviations from normal potassium carry risk of life-threatening arrhythmias. | Potassium < 3.5 mmol/L or > 5.0 mmol/L impairs cardiac conduction; severe abnormalities can cause cardiac arrest and are common in renal failure and shock. | Potassium < 3.5 mmol/L or > 5.0 mmol/L | high |
| Magnesium | Hypomagnesemia is frequently observed in the critically ill and linked to worse prognosis. | Low magnesium (<0.6 mmol/L or <1.5 mg/dL) promotes cardiac arrhythmias, neuromuscular irritability, and hypocalcemia, complicating the clinical course. | Magnesium < 0.6 mmol/L | medium |
| Urea_Nitrogen | Elevated blood urea nitrogen is a strong marker of renal dysfunction and catabolism. | BUN > 20 mg/dL (≈7.1 mmol/L) points to prerenal azotemia, renal failure, or gastrointestinal bleeding, all of which raise mortality. | Urea_Nitrogen > 20 mg/dL | high |
| inr | Prolonged INR is a powerful predictor of mortality. | INR > 1.5 indicates coagulopathy due to liver disease, DIC, or warfarin therapy, and is closely linked to bleeding risk and multi-organ failure. | INR > 1.5 | high |
| hemoglobin | Anemia significantly increases the risk of death in hospitalized patients. | Hemoglobin < 8 g/dL reduces oxygen delivery, exacerbates shock, and often necessitates transfusion; it signals chronic disease or acute blood loss. | hemoglobin < 8 g/dL | high |
| platelet | Thrombocytopenia is an independent risk factor for mortality. | Platelet count < 150 × 10⁹/L is a hallmark of sepsis-associated DIC, bone marrow suppression, or liver disease, and predicts bleeding and poor outcome. | platelet < 150 × 10⁹/L | high |
| wbc | Extreme white blood cell counts, both leukopenia and leukocytosis, predict higher mortality. | WBC < 4 or > 12 × 10⁹/L indicates severe infection, overwhelming sepsis (leukopenia is especially ominous), or hematologic stress; it is part of SIRS and SOFA criteria. | wbc < 4 × 10⁹/L or > 12 × 10⁹/L | high |
