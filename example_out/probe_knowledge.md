| Feature | Univariate signal (summary) | Clinical rationale | Suggested threshold | Evidence confidence (high/medium/low) |
|--------|-----------------------------|---------------------|---------------------|-------------------------------------|
| gender | Male sex is weakly to moderately associated with higher in-hospital mortality in many acute conditions. | Men may have higher comorbidity burden or different disease severity at presentation; sex-based physiological differences (e.g., immune response, cardiovascular reserve) contribute to slightly worse outcomes. | Male (binary) | medium |
| age | Older age is one of the strongest predictors of in-hospital death, with a near-linear increase in risk after 65 years. | Aging is linked to reduced physiological reserve, immunosenescence, frailty, and higher prevalence of multiple chronic conditions, all of which impair the ability to survive acute illness. | >65 years | high |
| Respiratory_failure | Presence of respiratory failure sharply increases mortality risk, often doubling or tripling the odds of death. | Inability to maintain adequate gas exchange leads to hypoxaemia, hypercapnia, acidosis and the need for invasive ventilation, directly driving organ dysfunction and death. | Present (1) | high |
| ARDS | Acute Respiratory Distress Syndrome carries a very high mortality risk (30–45% or more) in hospitalized patients. | ARDS causes severe, refractory hypoxaemia and is frequently accompanied by multi-organ failure, prolonged ventilation and complications such as ventilator-associated pneumonia. | Present (1) | high |
| sepsis | Sepsis is a dominant driver of in-hospital mortality; the presence of sepsis increases risk 5‑10 fold depending on onset and severity. | Dysregulated host response to infection leads to vasodilation, microcirculatory failure, organ hypoperfusion and a high risk of septic shock, which is a leading cause of death in ICUs. | Present (1) | high |
| Coronary_heart_disease | History of coronary heart disease moderately elevates in-hospital mortality risk, especially in patients with acute cardiovascular events. | Underlying coronary atherosclerosis predisposes to myocardial ischemia, arrhythmias, and heart failure during acute stress, critical illness, or major surgery. | Present (1) | high |
| Hypertension | Hypertension shows a modest univariate association with higher mortality, but its independent effect is attenuated after adjusting for age and other comorbidities. | Chronic hypertension contributes to end-organ damage (heart, kidney, brain), but the acute mortality link often reflects longer illness duration rather than direct causation. | Present (1) | medium |
| Diabetes | Diabetes consistently increases in-hospital mortality, particularly from infectious and cardiovascular causes. | Hyperglycaemia impairs immune function, delays wound healing, promotes thrombosis, and is associated with higher rates of sepsis and acute coronary syndromes. | Present (1) | high |
| Kidney_failure | Kidney failure (acute or chronic) is a strong, independent predictor of in-hospital death, with risk rising as renal function worsens. | Loss of renal clearance leads to fluid overload, electrolyte disturbances, uraemic toxicity, and difficulty managing medications; often signals multi-organ involvement. | Present (1) | high |
| Hyperlipidaemia | Hyperlipidaemia has an uncertain or paradoxically inverse association with short-term mortality (lower risk in some critically ill cohorts). | The “lipid paradox” suggests that higher cholesterol may reflect better nutritional status and lipoprotein anti-inflammatory properties, but causality is unclear; seldom a direct risk factor. | No clear threshold – presence may have no predictive value or weak protective signal. | low |
| COPD | Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease substantially increases mortality, especially when respiratory infection or acute exacerbation complicates the admission. | Reduced pulmonary reserve, impaired mucociliary clearance, chronic inflammation and frequent co-occurrence of right heart failure make ventilatory weaning difficult and raise death risk. | Present (1) | high |
| Cirrhosis | Cirrhosis is a very high‑risk condition with hospital mortality often >30% in acutely ill patients. | Portal hypertension, synthetic dysfunction, variceal bleeding, and high susceptibility to infections (spontaneous bacterial peritonitis) lead to rapid decompensation and multi-organ failure. | Present (1) | high |
| Hepatic_failure | Acute‑on‑chronic or acute liver failure carries one of the highest mortality risks among all comorbidities. | Severe hepatocyte loss causes coagulopathy, encephalopathy, hypoglycaemia, and circulatory dysfunction; without transplant, outcomes are very poor. | Present (1) | high |
| Fatty_liver | Fatty liver disease per se has a weak, if any, independent signal for in-hospital mortality; effects are often confounded by metabolic syndrome components. | Simple steatosis rarely causes acute decompensation; increased risk stems from its association with obesity, diabetes, and cardiovascular disease, not from fatty infiltration alone. | No clear threshold – presence alone is not a reliable predictor. | low |
| Acute_pancreatitis | Acute pancreatitis, particularly severe necrotising forms, raises hospital mortality substantially. | Systemic inflammatory response syndrome, pancreatic necrosis, organ failure (respiratory, renal, cardiovascular), and secondary infection drive a mortality rate of 10–30% in severe cases. | Present (1) | medium |
| Cerebral_infarction | Acute ischaemic stroke is associated with markedly elevated in-hospital mortality, driven by neurological deterioration and complications. | Large infarcts cause cerebral oedema, haemorrhagic transformation, aspiration pneumonia, and deep vein thrombosis; death often results from neurological injury or secondary sepsis. | Present (1) | high |
| ICH | Intracerebral haemorrhage is one of the deadliest acute conditions, with early mortality exceeding 30–50%. | Mass effect, intraventricular extension, and acute hydrocephalus rapidly raise intracranial pressure, causing herniation and brainstem death; surgical options are limited. | Present (1) | high |
| Malignancy | Active malignancy is a robust predictor of in-hospital death, particularly in the context of infection or surgery. | Immune suppression from cancer and chemotherapy, pro‑thrombotic state, malnutrition, and tumour‑related organ compromise (e.g., airway obstruction, marrow failure) greatly increase vulnerability. | Present (1) | high |
| Solidtumors | The presence of solid tumours (disseminated or advanced) is similarly linked to high mortality, often reflecting advanced disease and limited therapeutic options. | Large tumour burden causes local complications (bowel obstruction, respiratory failure) and paraneoplastic syndromes; patients are often poor surgical candidates with limited physiological reserve. | Present (1) | high |
| heart_rate | Both tachycardia and extreme bradycardia predict mortality; a heart rate >100 bpm is the most commonly observed risk marker in severe illness. | Tachycardia compensates for hypotension, fever, pain or hypovolaemia; sustained elevation indicates underlying shock, sepsis, or cardiac stress, while bradycardia (<50 bpm) may signal conduction system disease or terminal rhythm. | >100 bpm (or <50 bpm) – primary threshold for high risk is >100 bpm. | high |
| dbp | Low diastolic blood pressure is strongly associated with short–term death. | Diastolic hypotension reflects reduced vascular tone, hypovolaemia, or cardiogenic shock; it indicates inadequate coronary perfusion and imminent circulatory collapse. | <60 mmHg (severe risk <50 mmHg) | high |
| mbp | Mean blood pressure <65 mmHg is a well‑established criterion for hypotension requiring intervention. | Organ autoregulation fails below this threshold, leading to tissue hypoperfusion, lactic acidosis, and progressive organ failure; it is a core component of shock definitions. | <65 mmHg | high |
| resp_rate | Elevated respiratory rate (>22–24/min) is an early, sensitive marker of clinical deterioration and death. | Tachypnoea reflects respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis, hypoxaemia, or fever; a persistently high rate indicates high work of breathing and impending respiratory failure. | >24 breaths/min (sustained) | high |
| temperature | Both hypothermia (<36.0°C) and high fever (>38.5°C) are associated with increased mortality; hypothermia carries the strongest signal in sepsis. | Hypothermia indicates impaired thermoregulation in severe infection or shock; high fever reflects intense inflammatory response. No single threshold—both extremes must be considered. | No clear single threshold; <36.0°C and >38.5°C each indicate elevated risk. | high |
| SOFA | Sequential Organ Failure Assessment score is the reference standard for quantifying organ dysfunction; higher SOFA scores strongly predict death. | Each point increase in SOFA corresponds to a roughly linear rise in mortality risk; a change in SOFA ≥2 is used to define sepsis and marks a clinically significant worsening. | ≥2 points (compared to a baseline of 0) | high |
| calcium | Low ionised or total calcium (hypocalcemia) is frequently observed in critical illness and predicts poor outcomes. | Hypocalcemia arises from sepsis, pancreatitis, or alkalosis and contributes to neuromuscular irritability, myocardial depression, and coagulopathy; values <8.5 mg/dL (2.12 mmol/L) are concerning. | Total calcium <8.5 mg/dL (<2.12 mmol/L) | medium |
| bicarbonate | Low serum bicarbonate (metabolic acidosis) is a powerful marker of illness severity and risk of death. | Acidosis reflects lactate accumulation from shock, renal failure, or toxins; bicarbonate <22 mmol/L signals uncompensated acidosis and is a trigger for investigation of underlying shock. | <22 mmol/L | high |
| Chloride | Derangements in chloride (hypo- or hyper‑) are associated with increased mortality but are usually secondary to another process. | Severe hyperchloremia (>107 mmol/L) can contribute to acidosis but rarely acts alone; hypochloremia is seen in vomiting/diuretic use. The primary risk comes from the underlying disorder. | No clear threshold – often a marker of renal or acid‑base disturbance rather than a direct predictor. | low |
| Sodium | Both hyponatremia (<130 mmol/L) and hypernatremia (>150 mmol/L) are linked to higher in-hospital death, with a U‑shaped risk curve. | Extreme dysnatremias reflect severe neurohormonal dysregulation (SIADH, cerebral salt wasting, diabetes insipidus) and are markers of advanced underlying disease and brain injury. | No clear single threshold; <130 or >150 mmol/L both indicate high risk. | high |
| Potassium | Hyperkalemia (>5.5 mmol/L) and severe hypokalemia (<3.0 mmol/L) independently predict mortality, mainly via arrhythmic risk. | Hyperkalemia arises from renal failure, cell lysis, or acidosis, leading to fatal bradyarrhythmias; hypokalemia predisposes to ventricular ectopy and respiratory muscle weakness. | >5.5 mmol/L or <3.0 mmol/L – no single threshold covers all risk. | medium |
| Magnesium | Hypomagnesemia is associated with worse outcomes, especially in cardiovascular and septic patients. | Low magnesium exacerbates arrhythmias, vasospasm, and insulin resistance; levels <1.6 mg/dL (0.66 mmol/L) are often cited as a threshold for replacement and risk. | <1.6 mg/dL (0.66 mmol/L) | medium |
| Urea_Nitrogen | Elevated blood urea nitrogen is a strong, independent risk factor for death, reflecting both renal impairment and hypercatabolic states. | High BUN signals prerenal azotemia, acute tubular necrosis, or gastrointestinal bleeding; a level >20 mg/dL is a common cutoff used in mortality scores (e.g., CURB-65, APACHE). | >20 mg/dL | high |
| inr | Prolonged international normalized ratio (INR >1.5) indicates coagulopathy and strongly predicts death, especially in liver disease and sepsis. | Impaired synthesis of clotting factors due to liver failure or consumption in disseminated intravascular coagulation leads to bleeding and microthrombi, worsening organ failure. | >1.5 | high |
| hemoglobin | Anaemia (Hb <10 g/dL) is independently associated with higher mortality in hospitalised adults; extreme polycythaemia is rarer but also harmful. | Low haemoglobin reduces oxygen delivery, exacerbates tissue hypoxaemia, and often reflects malnutrition, chronic disease, or acute blood loss. | <10 g/dL (severe risk <7 g/dL) | medium |
| platelet | Thrombocytopenia (platelets <150,000/µL) is a well‑documented predictor of mortality in critical illness. | Low platelet count is a marker of DIC, bone marrow suppression, or immune destruction; it impairs haemostasis and is linked to bleeding and microvascular failure. | <150,000/µL | high |
| wbc | The white blood cell count has a U‑shaped association: both leukopenia (<4,000/µL) and leukocytosis (>12,000/µL) raise the risk of death. | Leukocytosis reflects infection, inflammation, or stress; leukopenia (often neutropenia) indicates overwhelming sepsis or bone marrow failure. The absolute risk is lowest within the normal range. | No clear single threshold; <4,000/µL or >12,000/µL both signal heightened risk. | high |
